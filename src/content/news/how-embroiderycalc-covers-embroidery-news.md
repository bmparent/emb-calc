“Embroidery in the news” can become a thin stream of rewritten announcements. That is not the goal here.

EmbroideryCalc will focus on developments that change how people understand, make, buy, preserve, or use embroidery—and explain why each development matters to a production shop or wearer.

## What belongs in the section

- manufacturer changes that affect thread, backing, equipment, or compatibility;
- standards, safety, labeling, trade, or technology developments with operational impact;
- research about materials, durability, automation, imaging, or color;
- cultural and historical case studies where embroidery carries public meaning;
- relevant business patterns, supported by identifiable data rather than anecdotes alone.

## Source first

Whenever possible, a piece will link to the original announcement, standard, paper, filing, dataset, institution, or manufacturer documentation. Secondary reporting can provide context, but it should not replace an available primary source.

The publication date and the date of the underlying event will be distinguished when that difference matters.

## Explain the practical consequence

A useful piece should answer at least one operational question: Does this change a material choice? A machine workflow? A customer conversation? A legal obligation? A maintenance assumption? A design trend worth watching?

If there is no practical or cultural consequence to explain, the item probably does not need an article.

## Keep inference visible

Facts from a source, EmbroideryCalc’s calculations, and editorial interpretation will be distinguishable. Uncertainty will be named. A manufacturer claim will be attributed rather than rewritten as an independent conclusion.

## Correct the record

Material corrections will update the article date and explain the change when context is useful. Readers can report a source problem or reproducible technical error through the project support channel.

## Separate commerce from the conclusion

An affiliate relationship or sponsor will be disclosed. It will not determine calculation results, color ranking, or whether a development is described positively. Source links remain source links; they do not become endorsements by proximity.

The best version of this section should help a busy decorator understand a changing field without turning the site into a press-release archive.
