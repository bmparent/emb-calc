RPM is a machine setting. Production speed is the outcome of a system.

A design running at a displayed 900 RPM does not automatically produce 54,000 good stitches in an hour. The machine accelerates and decelerates, changes direction, jumps, trims, changes colors, waits for an operator, and responds to the design and material.

## Effective RPM is the better planning number

EmbroideryCalc models an effective stitch rate with two factors:

`effective RPM = entered RPM × machine efficiency × design speed factor`

Machine efficiency represents the normal gap between the selected speed and sustained productive movement in your shop. The design speed factor lets one difficult location run below the rest of the job without changing the machine-wide assumption.

For example, a machine entered at 900 RPM with 85% efficiency and a 90% design factor is modeled at roughly 689 effective stitches per minute before explicit stops.

## What lowers the sustained rate

- tiny lettering and short, frequent movements;
- dense fills and abrupt direction changes;
- cap frames and difficult registration;
- fragile, specialty, or poorly tensioned thread;
- long jumps and frequent trims;
- color changes and applique stops;
- operator decisions made to protect quality;
- machine condition and maintenance.

The correct adjustment is not always “add more buffer.” If a design predictably stitches slower, reduce its design-speed factor. If the whole machine normally averages below the selected setting, adjust machine efficiency. Keep contingency for the variation you cannot model directly.

## Heads increase batch capacity, not needle speed

On a multi-head machine, each operating head can stitch a garment in the same batch. That can reduce the number of machine cycles needed for a quantity, but it does not make the design run faster at each needle.

A 12-head machine with 13 garments usually needs two batches, and the second batch uses only one head unless the schedule is reorganized. That incomplete batch is why dividing quantity by head count without rounding produces an optimistic answer.

## Measure your actual efficiency

Choose several representative jobs and record:

1. machine setting and design stitch count;
2. time when stitching actually begins and ends;
3. color-change, trim, bobbin, break, and operator-stop time;
4. garment, placement, thread, and design notes.

Separate predictable stops from needle movement. Then compare theoretical needle time with observed needle time. A median across representative jobs is usually a more stable starting point than one unusually clean or difficult run.

## When to change the profile

Change the shop-wide efficiency after repeated evidence. Use per-design factors for exceptions. This keeps one difficult cap from making every future left-chest estimate slower and prevents one unusually clean design from creating an unrealistic promise.

The goal is not the most flattering utilization number. It is a schedule the production floor can trust.
